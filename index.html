The Idea

What Frame actually does

AI video tools like Kling, Runway, and Sora are powerful — but they need very specific instructions to produce cinematic results. Vague prompts produce vague videos. Frame's job is to take your idea and turn it into the kind of precise visual instruction that these tools understand.

Think of it like this: You are the storyteller. Frame is the translator. You describe what you want to happen and feel — Frame converts that into the technical language the AI camera understands.

You do not need to know anything about filmmaking. You do not need to know what a "dolly shot" or "chiaroscuro lighting" means. You only need to know your story — Frame handles the translation.

The output format: Every prompt Frame produces is a single flowing paragraph — camera setup, character, action, movement, lighting, and color grade woven together as natural language. This integrated format consistently produces better AI video results than lists or bullet points.
How it works

Three steps.
That is all.

Open Frame. You will see a left column with three numbered steps. Fill them in order. Then hit Build Prompt.

1
Your story
Set once — applies to every scene in your film

Tell Frame what your film is about in plain, everyday language. There are three fields — the main idea, your main character, and the world or place the story happens in.

Write the way you would explain it to a friend. No need for film terms, no need for technical language. The more specific and honest your description, the better the prompt will be.

You write
Idea: A founder who spent 10 years building something nobody believed in — and the moment it finally works.

Character: Arjun, late 30s, worn jacket, tired eyes

World: Small workshop, late at night, fluorescent light
Frame understands
The emotional core of the story. Who the audience is watching. Where they are. This feeds into every scene — so every prompt feels like it belongs to the same film.
Tip: Don't overthink it. Two sentences for the idea, a name and one detail for the character, and a sense of place for the world is enough to start.
2
This scene
What you see happening in this specific moment

Describe what happens in this scene the way you would describe it to someone who can't see it. What is the character doing? What does the moment feel like? What does the environment look like?

You will also see a small dropdown in the top-right of this section — Open, Build, Turn, Resolve. This tells Frame where in the story this scene sits, which shapes how the prompt is written.

You write
He stands at the workbench, holding the finished product for the first time. His hands are shaking slightly. He doesn't smile — just stares at it like he can't believe it's real.
Frame reads
A moment of earned disbelief. Stillness. Weight. The kind of shot that needs to stay wide enough to show isolation, close enough to show hands.
Scene positions — what each one means
Open The first impression. Establish who your character is and where they live before anything happens. Sets the emotional tone.
Build The middle. Something is at stake. The character is working toward or struggling against something.
Turn The moment everything changes. A realization, a decision, a confrontation. The scene that makes the story matter.
Resolve The landing. The feeling after the turn. What the audience takes with them when the video ends.
3
Visual style
Pick one — Frame handles everything technical

Four style presets. Each one sets the entire visual direction — lens choice, lighting, camera movement, color grade, mood — automatically. You pick the feeling. Frame picks the technique.

Nolan
Feels like a thriller. Precise and psychological. Every frame earns its place. Shadows are deep. Silence is heavy. High contrast, film grain.
Use for: founder stories, moments of pressure, decisions, anything that needs weight and intensity.
Brand Film
Feels warm and human. Like an advertisement that doesn't feel like one. Close to the character. Soft light. Purposeful and honest.
Use for: brand stories, product launches, testimonials, anything where trust and warmth matter.
Epic
Feels massive. IMAX scale. The person looks small against the world. God rays through atmosphere. The kind of shot that makes an audience hold their breath.
Use for: landscapes, crowds, moments of awe, anything that needs to feel larger than one person.
Intimate
Feels close and real. Handheld, slightly unsteady. Warm single-source light. The feeling of being in the room with someone during a private moment.
Use for: emotional scenes, quiet turning points, vulnerability, anything where closeness matters more than scale.
Advanced controls — below the presets there is a collapsed section called "Advanced Director Controls". You can ignore this completely and still get great results. It is only for situations where you want to override a specific detail — for example, if you want Epic scale but with handheld camera movement instead of a crane shot.

Two modes

Build Prompt vs
Enhance with AI

Frame has two ways to generate a prompt. Both produce the same format. The difference is in the quality of the output and whether you need an API key.

◆ Template Built
Build Prompt
Instant. Works offline. No API key needed. Frame uses your story, scene idea, and style choice to fill a carefully written cinematic template. The result is always correctly formatted and ready to use.
Use this when: you want a result immediately, you are testing ideas, you don't have an API key yet, or the template result is already good enough for your needs.
✦ AI Enhanced
Enhance with AI
Uses a live AI model to write a custom prompt around your specific story, character, and scene idea. The result is more natural, more specific to your exact situation, and more creatively varied.
Use this when: you want the highest quality result, you need the prompt to reflect very specific story details, or the template result feels too generic for your project.

How to get an API key (it's free)

An API key is like a personal password that connects Frame directly to an AI provider. Frame supports three providers — all are free to start. Your key is stored only in your own browser and sent directly from your device to the provider. Frame never sees it.

1
Choose a provider
Anthropic (recommended): console.anthropic.com — free to start
Google Gemini: aistudio.google.com — free tier available
OpenAI: platform.openai.com — paid only
2
Sign up and create a key
Create a free account. Go to the API keys section. Click "Create new key". Copy the key — it will look something like: sk-ant-api03-…
3
Paste it in Frame
In the right panel of Frame, find the "AI Connection" card. Paste your key. Click "Verify & Connect". Frame checks the key works, then saves it in your browser. Done — you can now use Enhance with AI for every scene.
4
Your key stays on your device
The key lives in your browser's local storage. It is never sent to any server other than the AI provider directly. If you want to use Frame on a shared device, uncheck "Remember device" — it will only last until you close the browser tab.
Getting better results

Tips that make a
real difference

✍
Write your scene idea in first person
Instead of "a man walks into a room", try "he walks into the room slowly, like he's not sure he should be there". Specific detail produces specific video.
🎭
Describe emotion, not action
"His hands are shaking" tells the AI what to show. "He is nervous" tells it nothing specific. Show the physical sign of the feeling — not the label.
🎬
One clear thing per scene
The best AI videos show one moment, not three. If you have multiple things happening, split them into multiple scenes. Each scene gets its own prompt.
🔄
Regenerate until it feels right
The first result is not always the best one. Hit Regenerate to get a different version of the same scene. Lock a prompt you love to protect it.
📋
Copy and paste directly
Once your prompt is generated, click Copy Prompt and paste it directly into Kling, Runway, or Sora. No editing needed — the format is ready as-is.
💾
Your work saves automatically
Frame saves everything as you type — story, scenes, generated prompts. Close the tab and come back later — everything will be exactly where you left it.

Before you go

The one thing that
matters most

The quality of your prompt is determined almost entirely by the quality of your scene description in Step 2. The more specific, honest, and visual your description, the better the result — regardless of which style you pick or whether you use AI enhancement.

The test: Read your scene description out loud. Can you see it? Can you hear it? Does it feel like something that actually happened to a real person? If yes — your prompt will be good. If it still feels abstract or general — go back and add one more specific physical detail.

You do not need to master filmmaking to use Frame. You need to know your story. Frame does the rest.
